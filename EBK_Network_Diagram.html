The Closed Loop

1. Single-Counterparty Ecosystem

Pax-Bank's €8.1B balance sheet has one client: the Catholic Church. Every loan, every deposit, every payment — all within the same canonical hierarchy. A corporate analyst would call this Klumpenrisiko.

2. Circular Guarantees

The diocese guarantees CBT/ASW/VKKD bank debt. The bank that holds the debt is owned by... the diocese. If a borrower defaults, the loss hits the bank, whose largest depositor is the guarantor. Risk never transfers — it just rotates.

3. Governance Conflict

Gordon Sobbeck sits on Pax-Bank Aufsichtsrat (lender) AND CBT Verwaltungsrat (borrower). Same individual oversees both sides of a €66M lending relationship. Also on ASW, Aachener GV, and RWB boards.

4. Banking Secrecy Shield

§26 KWG protects Pax-Bank's loan book from external access. This loan book IS the consolidated debt schedule of the entire church. The one document that would reveal everything is legally shielded.

5. Accounting Arbitrage

Each entity files independently under HGB. Nobody consolidates. The diocese's cooperative shares in Pax-Bank are booked at cost (~€5M) while the economic value of its stake in a €560M-equity bank is ~€110M. Parish property at €1 per building. €1.9B KZVK liability on nobody's books.

Single Point of Failure

Kirchensteuer Dependency

The entire loop depends on one cash inflow: €800M/yr Kirchensteuer. Church membership declining 3–4%/yr. Every entity — diocese, bank, hospitals, parishes — is correlated to the same variable. No diversification, no external capital, no independent oversight.
